Rohini (1980)


The Rohini series of satellites were part of India's indigenously developed satellite launch program. The Rohini satellite series began with the launch of Rohini 1 in 1980.

Rohini 1 was launched on July 18, 1980.The launch vehicle used for this mission was the Satellite Launch Vehicle (SLV-3), which was an Indian-made launch vehicle.The primary purpose of the Rohini series was to demonstrate India's capability to design, build, and launch satellites into space using indigenous launch vehicles.The payload of Rohini 1 included scientific instruments for conducting experiments in the fields of aeronomy and solar physics.Rohini 1 was placed in a low Earth orbit.The mission was successful, marking India's first satellite to be placed in orbit by an Indian-made launch vehicle.The success of Rohini 1 was followed by additional missions in the Rohini series, including Rohini 2, Rohini 3, and so on. These missions aimed at furthering India's space capabilities and conducting scientific experiments.The development and launch of the Rohini series played a crucial role in advancing India's space program and laying the foundation for subsequent satellite launch vehicles, such as the Polar Satellite Launch Vehicle (PSLV) and the Geosynchronous Satellite Launch Vehicle (GSLV).The successful launch of Rohini 1 demonstrated India's progress in space technology and its ability to independently launch satellites into space.





INSAT series (1983 onwards)


The Indian National Satellite System (INSAT) is a series of multipurpose geostationary satellites developed and operated by the Indian Space Research Organisation (ISRO). The INSAT series has been in operation since 1983 and continues to play a crucial role in providing a variety of services for India and neighboring regions. Here are some key points about the INSAT series:

The INSAT series started with the launch of INSAT-1B in 1983. Subsequent satellites have been launched to replace aging ones and expand the capabilities of the system.The series includes both communication and meteorological satellites.INSAT satellites are placed in geostationary orbits, meaning they orbit the Earth at the same rotational speed as the planet. This allows them to remain fixed over a specific geographic area, providing continuous coverage.INSAT satellites are primarily used for providing a wide range of communication services, including television broadcasting, telecommunication, and broadband services.The satellites facilitate direct-to-home (DTH) broadcasting, VSAT (Very Small Aperture Terminal) communications, and other telecommunication applications.Some INSAT satellites are equipped with meteorological instruments, providing valuable data for weather monitoring and forecasting.These satellites contribute to the monitoring of cyclones, weather patterns, and other meteorological phenomena in the Indian subcontinent and surrounding regions.






IRS series (1988 onwards)

The Indian Remote Sensing (IRS) satellite program, initiated in 1988 by the Indian Space Research Organisation (ISRO), aims to provide Earth observation data for various applications, including agriculture, forestry, water resources, urban planning, and disaster management. Here are some key points about the IRS series:

The IRS program began with the launch of IRS-1A in 1988. Since then, multiple satellites in the series have been launched, each carrying advanced remote sensing instruments.The IRS satellites have been developed to enhance India's capabilities in Earth observation and resource monitoring.IRS satellites are equipped with multispectral and hyperspectral imaging instruments that capture data in different wavelengths of the electromagnetic spectrum. This allows for detailed analysis of Earth's surface features.The IRS satellites support a wide range of applications, including agriculture monitoring, forestry management, soil mapping, water resource management, urban planning, and disaster management.The high-resolution imagery provided by these satellites enables precise and detailed observations, aiding in decision-making processes.

Mangalyaan (2013)

Mangalyaan, officially known as the Mars Orbiter Mission (MOM), is India's first interplanetary mission, specifically designed to explore Mars. Launched by the Indian Space Research Organisation (ISRO), Mangalyaan was a historic mission for India, marking its entry into the exploration of the Red Planet. Here are key details about the Mangalyaan mission:

Mangalyaan was launched on November 5, 2013. The launch vehicle used for Mangalyaan was the Polar Satellite Launch Vehicle (PSLV-C25).Mangalyaan's primary objectives were to develop technologies required for interplanetary missions and to explore Mars' surface, morphology, atmosphere, and mineralogy. The mission aimed to demonstrate India's capability to design, plan, and execute an interplanetary mission on a relatively modest budget.The Mangalyaan spacecraft had five scientific instruments to study various aspects of Mars, including its surface, atmosphere, and mineral composition. Instruments included a color camera, methane sensor, thermal infrared spectrometer, Lyman-Alpha Photometer, and Mars Exospheric Neutral Composition Analyzer.Mangalyaan gained international attention for being one of the most cost-effective Mars missions ever undertaken. It achieved its objectives at a fraction of the cost of similar missions by other space agencies.On September 24, 2014, Mangalyaan successfully entered Mars' orbit, making India the first Asian nation to reach Martian orbit and the first nation in the world to do so in its maiden attempt.Initially planned for six months, Mangalyaan continued to function well beyond its expected operational life. As of my last knowledge update in January 2022, the orbiter was still operational and sending valuable data.The success of Mangalyaan garnered international acclaim and highlighted India's growing capabilities in space exploration. ISRO became the first space agency to succeed in a Mars mission on its maiden attempt.





Chandrayaan-1 (2008)

Chandrayaan-1 was India's first lunar probe, launched by the Indian Space Research Organisation (ISRO) in 2008. It was a significant milestone in India's space exploration program and marked the country's entry into lunar exploration. Here are key details about the Chandrayaan-1 mission:

Chandrayaan-1 was launched on October 22, 2008. The launch vehicle used for Chandrayaan-1 was the Polar Satellite Launch Vehicle (PSLV-C11).Chandrayaan-1 had multiple scientific objectives, including mapping the lunar surface, studying the mineral composition of the Moon, and searching for water molecules in the lunar soil. The mission aimed to increase understanding of the Moon's geological and topographical features.The Chandrayaan-1 spacecraft carried a suite of scientific instruments, including a Moon Impact Probe (MIP) that was released and impacted the lunar surfaceOne of the notable discoveries made by Chandrayaan-1 was the presence of water molecules on the Moon. The Moon Impact Probe detected water molecules on the lunar surface.Unfortunately, the mission faced an early termination. The communication with Chandrayaan-1 was lost in August 2009, just 10 months into the mission. Despite the communication loss, the data collected during the operational phase contributed significantly to lunar science.Chandrayaan-1 featured international collaboration, including instruments from NASA, ESA (European Space Agency), and other space agencies.Chandrayaan-1 laid the foundation for future lunar exploration missions by India and other countries. The success of Chandrayaan-1 demonstrated India's growing capabilities in space exploration and enhanced its reputation in the global space community.





NavIC (2016 onwards)

NavIC, which stands for Navigation with Indian Constellation, is an independent regional navigation satellite system developed by the Indian Space Research Organisation (ISRO). It is designed to provide accurate and reliable positioning and timing information over India and the surrounding region. Here are key details about the NavIC mission:

The first satellite in the NavIC constellation, IRNSS-1A, was launched on July 1, 2013. The NavIC constellation consists of a total of seven satellites, and it became operational in April 2018.The primary objective of NavIC is to provide accurate and reliable position information over India and the surrounding region, extending up to 1,500 kilometers from the country's borders.NavIC provides two types of navigation signals: Standard Positioning Service (SPS) for civilian users and Restricted Service (RS) for authorized users such as the military.NavIC provides coverage in the Indian Ocean region and extends up to 1,500 kilometers from India's borders. The system is designed to offer accurate positioning and timing information in real-time.NavIC has various applications, including terrestrial, aerial, and marine navigation, disaster management, vehicle tracking, and location-based services. It enhances the accuracy and reliability of GPS-derived information in the region.The NavIC constellation consists of seven satellites: IRNSS-1A, IRNSS-1B, IRNSS-1C, IRNSS-1D, IRNSS-1E, IRNSS-1F, and IRNSS-1G.NavIC provides regional autonomy in navigation, reducing dependence on foreign navigation satellite systems and enhancing the security and strategic capabilities of India.NavIC is designed to work in conjunction with other global navigation satellite systems like GPS (Global Positioning System), providing users in the region with improved accuracy and reliability.





Chandrayaan-2 (2019)

Chandrayaan-2 is India's second lunar exploration mission, following the success of Chandrayaan-1 in 2008. Launched by the Indian Space Research Organisation (ISRO) in 2019, Chandrayaan-2 was an ambitious mission with an orbiter, lander, and rover components. Here are key details about the Chandrayaan-2 mission:

Chandrayaan-2 was launched on July 22, 2019. The launch vehicle used for Chandrayaan-2 was the Geosynchronous Satellite Launch Vehicle Mark III (GSLV Mk III).The mission aimed to study the lunar surface, map its topography, mineral composition, and exosphere, and explore the presence of water molecules. The orbiter carried a suite of scientific instruments to carry out remote sensing observations.The landing site for Vikram (the lander) was near the Moon's south pole, an area not explored by previous missions. This region is of particular interest due to the presence of permanently shadowed areas where water ice could potentially exist.While the orbiter successfully entered lunar orbit, the lander Vikram experienced a loss of communication during its descent, resulting in an unsuccessful landing attempt.Despite the anomaly, the orbiter continued to function and gather valuable scientific data.The Chandrayaan-2 orbiter has continued to function well beyond its originally planned mission duration. As of my last knowledge update in January 2022, the orbiter was still operational and sending data.Chandrayaan-2 featured international collaboration, including instruments from NASA, such as the Laser Retroreflector Array (LRA) installed on the lander.The mission, despite the lander anomaly, has made significant contributions to lunar science by providing detailed observations and data from the orbiter.





Aditya-L1

Aditya-L1 is a mission by the Indian Space Research Organisation (ISRO) dedicated to studying the Sun. The primary objective of the Aditya-L1 mission is to enhance our understanding of the Sun and its impact on space weather. Here are some key details about the Aditya-L1 mission:

As of my last knowledge update in January 2022, Aditya-L1 had not been launched. The mission was anticipated to be launched in the future.The primary scientific objectives of Aditya-L1 include studying the outermost layer of the Sun, known as the corona, and understanding the dynamics of solar activities such as solar flares and Coronal Mass Ejections (CMEs).The mission aims to investigate the Sun's magnetic field and its influence on the solar wind and the Earth's climate.Aditya-L1 is expected to carry a suite of scientific instruments designed to observe and analyze various aspects of solar activities. The payload may include instruments such as a visible emission line coronagraph, ultraviolet (UV) spectrometer, and other tools for solar observations.Aditya-L1 is planned to be placed in a halo orbit around the first Lagrangian point (L1), which is a gravitational equilibrium point located between the Earth and the Sun. This orbit allows for continuous observations of the Sun without the interference of the Earth. The data collected by Aditya-L1 is expected to contribute to improved space weather prediction capabilities, helping to mitigate the impact of solar activities on communication, navigation systems, and power grids on Earth.The Aditya-L1 mission involves collaboration with international space agencies and organizations to enhance scientific cooperation and share data for a better understanding of solar processes.
